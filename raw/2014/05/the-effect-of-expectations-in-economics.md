I started out [a post a few days ago](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/05/expectations-destroy-information.html) with saying I had a beef with use of expectations in economics that I thought I'd flesh out a bit in light of some comments from [Tom Brown and Mark Sadowski](http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=26552&cpage=2#comment-328760). I didn't mean to imply that expectations didn't have an effect on the direction of prices. The beef was with the use of expectations in theoretical models in economics. The particular case I called out was [inflation expectations](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/04/inflation-predictions-are-hard.html) -- a theory including measured expectations was no more predictive of inflation than a simple martingale that used past inflation to predict future inflation. In fact, the expectations model was systematically inaccurate. 



The point of my post was to show that systematic inaccuracy leads to information loss which translates into a negative impact on the economy. However I didn't emphasize enough that it doesn't matter the sign of the inaccuracy -- even over-optimism should lead to information loss and a negative impact on the economy. The systematic high inflation expectations above could lead to sub-par NGDP growth relative to the fundamentals. In fact, ever since inflation reached a peak in the late 1970s in many industrialized countries, people have expected higher inflation than actually occurred and NGDP growth has been lower (I'm not saying this is the correct reason for this; there are many different explanations).



Mark Sadowski [linked](http://andy.egge.rs/papers/Eggers_Fouirnaies_Recessions.pdf) \[pdf\] in his comment to a report that showed an impact of expectations of uninformed vs informed consumers based on a recession being "called". Essentially when a recession is called, even if it is just a "red zero" where it could have easily been negative because of random chance (not fundamentals), it creates a disproportionate negative impact on consumer spending by "uninformed consumers". In the information transfer model we'd explain this by saying some subset of the population updated their probabilities of expected growth rate incorrectly, leading to information loss, creating a negative impact on the economy. This is just another example of being incorrectly pessimistic about the fundamentals leading to negative impact.



The interesting cut of the beef, and the one that diverges from mainstream economic thought, is where inaccurate optimism about growth **_also_** leads to lower growth. This has particular relevance to monetarist theories purporting that more effective central bank communication could lead to a greater impact of policies such as quantitative easing (even allowing less monetary expansion to achieve the same effect, see e.g. [here](http://macromarketmusings.blogspot.com/2014/03/ad-hoc-monetary-policy.html)). Is the sub-par growth in the post-financial crisis period due to expectation of recovery? Maybe. Maybe it's just [secular stagnation](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/11/secular-stagnation-and-eu.html).



This idea that incorrect expectations lead to a negative impact regardless if they are positive or negative jibes with Milton Friedman's "[plucking model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/12/this-plucking-model.html)" where there is a natural path of e.g. NGDP and deviations are all negative deviations.



This idea that there are only negative deviations traces back to the idea in the information transfer model that the information in the demand (D) represents a bound on the amount of information transferred so that the information received by the supply (S) is always less, i.e. I(S) ≤ I(D). We call the case of equality "ideal information transfer" and inequality "non-ideal". [Solving some differential equations](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/04/sticky-prices-from-non-ideal.html) and using [Gronwall's inequality](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gronwall%27s_inequality) leads us to the following supply and demand diagram:



![](<media/ite expectations supply and demand.png>)



The solid supply and demand curves represent ideal information transfer intersecting at the ideal price P\*, and the real world price P would appear somewhere in the lower triangle. If the difference between the ideal and non-ideal cases was stable, the price would follow the dotted supply and demand curves. The key takeaway is that the price P always falls below the theoretical model for the ideal case.
