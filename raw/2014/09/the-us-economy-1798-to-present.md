[Tom Brown](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/09/what-is-inflation.html?showComment=1410652217970#c272141749698223826) directed me to [David Andolfatto's post](http://andolfatto.blogspot.com/2014/09/whos-afraid-of-deflation.html) about deflation; I am still pondering that post and plan on discussing it in the future. However, one thing that caught my eye is that Andolfatto cites [measuringworth.com](http://www.measuringworth.com/index.php) (MW) which has produced some estimates for the US economy going back to the late 18th century. My question is, as always, how does [the information transfer model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/06/the-information-transfer-model.html) (ITM) do with this data?



One thing that is missing is currency data going back to the 1790s. That turns out not to be a big problem -- if we use the interest rate data from MW and trust the ITM interest rate model. This interest rate model seems to work right through monetary regime changes (see e.g. [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/exit-through-hyperinflation.html) or [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/11/the-long-run-in-uk.html)), so I will use it to estimate the currency base (M0) based on NGDP and interest rate data from MW. One thing that we can immediately show after extrapolating the currency base is the path of the US economy through NGDP-M0 space:



![](<media/ite long run in the us 3.png>)

And one thing that immediate jumps out of this graph are two trend paths (derived from the information transfer model for a [large number of random markets](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/06/the-macroeconomic-partition-function.html)). One holds while the US is on the gold standard and a second picks up after WWII. The three major upward deviations from the trend preceding major crashes are associated with the War of 1812 (the 1815-1821 depression), the Civil War (the 1865-1867 recession) and the Great Depression. On this scale, most of the post-war recessions of the fiat currency regime look insignificant (even the "Great Recession"). See [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/can-information-theory-predict.html) for more about how these deviations are associated with recessions.

Overall, the mode does a pretty good job -- the ITM has somewhat less dramatic shifts in in inflation (which leads to some systematic overestimation/underestimation of the price level since the price level depends on the integral of inflation). I dealt with the missing part in during the transition off of the gold standard [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/exit-through-hyperinflation.html) -- the annual data is too coarse to get that piece to be anything more than a line.
