Part of doing research is just trying things. So I tried looking at the prime age employment-population ratio in terms of the [dynamic information equilibrium model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/01/dynamic-equilibrium-unemployment-rate.html). It works a bit, but also fails a bit. And the failure might be interesting. It works well from the 1990s to the present:



![](<media/unemployment ratio -log version- EPOP ratio 1988 to 2017.png>)
![](<media/unemployment ratio -log version- EPOP ratio 1988 to 2017 2.png>)

However, before that time it's much more complex (I didn't even bother fitting the shocks, just extended the the fit to 1987-2017 back to the 1950s):



![](<media/unemployment ratio -log version- EPOP ratio to 1988.png>)

The interesting thing is that this was a period when many more people were entering the workforce in the US, especially [women](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/LNS12300002). In fact, if we look at [men alone](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/LNS12300001) the dynamic equilibrium works great from the 1960s to the present:



![](<media/unemployment ratio -log version- EPOP ratio men 1.png>)
![](<media/unemployment ratio -log version- EPOP ratio men 2.png>)

Women entering the workforce is a strong non-equilibrium effect on the employment population ratio \[see below\], just like how women (potentially) [impacted inflation](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/01/is-cpi-information-theoretic-measure-of.html) (the high inflation in the 1970s was caused by [demographics, not monetary policy](http://www.interfluidity.com/v2/4561.html)).
