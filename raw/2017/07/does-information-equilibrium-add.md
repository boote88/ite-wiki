I've been asked several times about the utility of the forecasts I make with the information equilibrium (IE) model. I came up with a good way to quantify it for the US 10 year interest rate forecast [I made nearly 2 years ago](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/comparison-of-interest-rate-predictions.html). Here I've compared the IE model (free) to both the [Blue Chip Economic Indicators](https://lrus.wolterskluwer.com/product-family/blue-chip) (BCEI, for which the editors [charge almost 1500 dollars per year](https://lrus.wolterskluwer.com/store/products/blue-chip-economic-indicators-prod-000000000010041428/journal-item-1-000000000010041428)) and a simple [ARIMA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoregressive_integrated_moving_average) process:


![](<media/ITM interest rates and price level -prediction comparison-.png>)

As you can see, the IE model represents a considerable restriction of the uncertainty relative to the ARIMA model (which is to say it adds bits of information/erases bits of randomness ‒ which explains the bad pun in the title of this post).
