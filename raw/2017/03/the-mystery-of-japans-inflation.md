[Noah Smith has said](https://www.bloomberg.com/view/articles/2016-03-10/an-economics-laboratory-where-theories-go-to-die) that Japan is where macroeconomic theories go to die (well, except [this one](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/03/im-not-quite-dead-sir.html)), and when I initially looked at Japan's price level it with the [dynamic equilibrium](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/01/dynamic-equilibrium-presentation.html) model (more [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/03/the-quantity-theory-of-labor-and.html)) it looked grim. However, [as the Dude says](https://memegenerator.net/instance/59593557/big-lebowski-my-thinking-about-this-case-had-become-very-uptight), my thinking about this case had become very uptight. While Japan would not be a good example of the dynamic equilibrium framework on its own, given the framework works for other inflation data (e.g. [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/02/nairu-and-other-connections-between.html) or [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/03/three-centuries-of-dynamic-equilibria.html)) we can be a bit more assured that the giant ongoing shock required to fit the price level data isn't spurious. It still could be spurious, but at least it's not a "surely you're joking" _ad hoc_ hypothesis.

Note that I removed the [effects of the VAT increases](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/dont-forget-vat.html) from the data. The shocks are centered at 1974.6 (positive) and 2006.5 (negative). What's also interesting is that the width (similar to the standard deviation of a normal distribution) of the latter shock is 108 months (9 years) which means it won't be over for many years to come. Instead of "Abenomics", the recent rising/stabilizing inflation is the ending of the giant "lost decade" shock.



Of course, this picture also makes a bold claim that the equilibrium rate of inflation is 4.3% per year (± 0.4 percentage points at 2σ).
