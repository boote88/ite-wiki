Alex Tabarrok [cites a study](http://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2015/10/the-future-is-here-just-not-evenly-distributed.html) that says productivity may not be growing overall, but is growing for firms on the "global productivity frontier". It does seem a bit strange to select a subset consisting of the most productive firms and then saying these firms have high productivity growth. While not exactly contradictory (it's a finding that high productivity "stock" has high productivity "flow"), it does seem like they should go a bit farther in figuring out if this partitioning of the data makes sense.



In any case, the result isn't particularly shocking if we go back to [the dark matter problem](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/04/solving-dark-matter-problem.html). Selecting the most productive firms is very much like the selection process going into selecting firms for a stock index. You end up over-representing the firms with large information transfer index values.
