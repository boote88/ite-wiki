I've been home with a cold the past couple of days and was inspired by [Simon Wren-Lewis's post on recent GDP data](http://mainlymacro.blogspot.com/2015/03/a-quick-note-on-two-recoveries.html) out of the UK to update the results (which I hadn't done with contemporary data [in awhile](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/02/australia-uk-and-eu.html)).



In doing so, I realized that the fit to the price level didn't actually work for CPI data after 2008 -- the discrepancy is so large that it hints at the possibility of [monetary regime change](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/monetary-regime-change.html) in the UK. However, there does not seem to be anything that the UK did that was different from the US in terms of monetary policy -- both nations conducted QE, bailed out banks, and neither devalued their currency though an exchange rate peg. Why then would the US not have a regime change while the UK did? The UK switched from an inflation target range in 1997 (and to CPI from a retail price index in 2003). Basically, this could be construed as evidence against the information transfer model requiring an _ad hoc_ monetary phase transition to fit the data.



Any other ideas? Am I missing something?
