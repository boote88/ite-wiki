An [anonymous commenter](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/is-human-agency-noahs-big-unchallenged.html?showComment=1441216948954#c8583768505241881304) brought up [my Canada model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/06/celebrating-500-posts-with-predictive.html) yesterday apropos of Canada "officially" going into a recession. [Scott Sumner](http://econlog.econlib.org/archives/2015/09/pseudo_recessio.html) has some issues with the designation (calling it a pseudo-recession), something I've responded to [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/12/japans-new-recession.html).



Coincidentally, I was preparing a post in response to [Nick Rowe's question](http://worthwhile.typepad.com/worthwhile_canadian_initi/2015/08/why-did-canadian-inflation-targeting-work-in-1996-but-fail-in-2008.html) of why inflation targeting worked in 1996 in Canada but not 2008 (or 2015).



There is a simple reason in the information equilibrium model. The central bank can target any more inflation rate up to a rate _π₀_ (I put the model results this way [once before](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/zen-koan-inflation-targeting.html)). That rate _π₀_ steadily falls (unless there are large supply shocks like the oil shocks of 1973/4 and 1979 or hyperinflation intervening).



If your target is _π_ then there are two scenarios:

-   _π < π₀_ : Inflation targeting works fine
-   _π > π₀_ : Inflation targeting doesn't work because π is not achievable.

Canada was once (in 1996) well in the _π < π₀_ regime. Now it's nearer _π ~ π₀_. Therefore 1996 worked out (no recession), but 2008 did not. And now we have 2015.



However, I have just shifted Nick's question to:

> _Why does π₀ fall?_

As [I like to say](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/05/what-is-economic-growth.html) there are more ways an economy can be organized when it is made up of lots of low growth states than a few high growth states. That is to say it is increasingly likely that a given dollar will be facilitating a transaction in a low growth industry.



Low growth goes hand in hand with low inflation and a large economy is more likely to be made up of many slowly growing prices than a few quickly growing ones.



All of these statements are basically saying a larger economy has higher entropy than a smaller one.
