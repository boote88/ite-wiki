The SNB pegged their currency to the Euro in order to devalue it, but they still had deflation (or really low inflation). It did move the exchange rate -- which [Scott Sumner](http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=28389) thinks is a sufficient indication is an indicator \[[Sumner disagreed](http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=28389#comment-375880) with that characterization, however see [here](http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=28054)\*\*\] for looser monetary policy. 






-   [Switzerland is in a liquidity trap](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/monetary-regime-change.html)
-   [Exchange rates don't have an immediate link to inflation](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/09/what-do-exchange-rates-measure.html)



The [SNB has decided](http://www.snb.ch/en/mmr/reference/pre_20150115/source/pre_20150115.en.pdf) to let their currency appreciate again after the devaluation failed to produce inflation or spur growth -- as would be predicted by a liquidity trap model.

"BTW, Kuroda is engaged in monetary offset (the yen has recently fallen from 109 to 118)" 



I would say engaging in monetary offset is looser policy; tighter policy would stand to see a rise in the Yen.
