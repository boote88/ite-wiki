Except for the price level of capital being strange (and not saying it really is the price level of capital [anyway](https://research.stlouisfed.org/fred2/series/PLKCPPGBA670NRUG), so I used the [GDP deflator](https://research.stlouisfed.org/fred2/series/GBRGDPDEFQISMEI)), this is a run of [this model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/03/a-quantity-theory-of-labor-and-capital.html) for the UK. It does reasonably well (at least better than [this model](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/01/its-people-economy-is-made-out-of-people.html), which doesn't work at all), but misses out on the great recession. Maybe it was more concentrated in the financial sector in the UK? Maybe it was more [non-ideal information transfer](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/03/non-ideal-information-transfer-tail.html) than in e.g. the US? (These two questions are related in the IE model.)

_CPI : NGDP ⇄ L
  X : NGDP ⇄ K_

The _X_ is an irrelevant price in the capital market. We can solve these markets for the general equilibrium (as in the [paper](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/information-equilibrium-as-economic.html)):



_NGDP = n0 (L/l0)ᵝ (K/k0)ᵞ

CPI ≡ dNGDP/dL = (n0/l0) β (L/l0)ᵝ⁻¹(K/k0)ᵞ_

The information transfer indices are empirically _β_ = 0.4 and _γ_ = 1.0 (compared to the US, which were _β_ \= 0.8 and _γ_ = 0.7).
