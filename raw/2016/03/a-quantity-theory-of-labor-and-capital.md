Some of the problems with the "[quantity theory of labor](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/01/its-people-economy-is-made-out-of-people.html)" is 1) it doesn't work for the UK, and 2) it underestimates RGDP.



What about the Solow model? It was [very accurate](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/12/the-information-transfer-solow-growth.html) as a model of NGDP. And it is [accurate for UK](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/05/the-uk-another-case-of-productivity.html). Let me lay out the model. Consider two simultaneous markets (employed labor and nominal capital) with core CPI being the detector in the labor market:



_CPI : NGDP ⇄ L_
   _X : NGDP ⇄ K_


The _X_ is an irrelevant price in the capital market \[_ed. note_: actually, it's technically the capital deflator\]. We can solve these markets for the general equilibrium (as in the [paper](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/information-equilibrium-as-economic.html)):



_NGDP = n0 (L/l0)ᵝ (K/k0)ᵞ_

_CPI ≡ dNGDP/dL = (n0/l0) β (L/l0)ᵝ⁻¹(K/k0)ᵞ_


The only other detail is that I used core CPI to inflate real capital to obtain nominal capital (the result does not change much if you use e.g. the GDP deflator). Capital stock only goes until 2012 [on FRED](https://research.stlouisfed.org/fred2/series/RKNANPUSA666NRUG) as well.



So how does this perform? In a word, **brilliantly**:

Oh, wait -- so does this help us with why the quantity theory of labor performs poorly for the UK? Yes, it does. The UK (in this model) is much more capital intensive than the US or Canada. In the US (above), we have _β_ = 0.8 and _γ_ = 0.7, but [the UK](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/05/the-uk-another-case-of-productivity.html) has _β_ = 0.5 and _γ_ = 1.0 -- meaning the capital component contains much more of the variation.









Also the IT Solow model doesn't require _β_ + _γ_ = 1.
