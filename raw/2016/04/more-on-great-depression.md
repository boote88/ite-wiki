[David Glasner](https://uneasymoney.com/2016/04/13/whats-so-bad-about-the-gold-standard/) has a new post up on the monetary theory of the Great Depression which reminded me that I could do a bit more in the vein of [this post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/04/fiscal-stimulus-in-great-depression.html). I'm sure the data is probably consistent with several different causal mechanisms. My only (interesting) opinion about the gold standard was that leaving the gold standard [wasn't relevant to the recovery](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/01/gold-was-irrelevant.html), but rather [interest rate pegging](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/03/bernanke-endorses-policy-recommednation.html) driving (effective) hyperinflation.



Anyway, I grabbed the FRED series A08170USA175NNBR (non-agricultural employment 1900-1943) and tested the model that nominal shocks were proportional to labor supply shocks (that I talked about in [this post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2015/08/employment-doesnt-depend-of-inflation.html))

Unfortunately due to the low resolution of the data, I can't quite see [if this mechanism is at play](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/are-interest-rates-good-indicator-of.html) -- that the [Fed raised interest rates](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/09/the-emerging-story-of-great-recession.html) (see [here](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/08/improved-estimate-of-pre-depression.html) also) and caused an "[avalanche](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/03/the-monetary-base-as-sand-pile.html)". Raising interest rates (effective monetary tightening) in the run-up to the market crash of 1929 caused the subsequent crash (similar to the [effective rise in interest rates](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/02/the-fed-caused-great-recession.html) in the run-up to the Great Recession), like an [airplane stalling to lose altitude](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2014/09/another-analogy-for-monetary-policy-and.html).





![](<media/interest rates.png>)

And the tightening by the Fed started happening as early as the 1920s, much like the [Fed tightening starting in the mid-2000s](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/02/2006.html).
