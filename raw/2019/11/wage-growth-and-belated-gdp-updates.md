Wage growth data [from the Atlanta Fed](https://www.frbatlanta.org/chcs/wage-growth-tracker.aspx?panel=1) came out yesterday and the [dynamic information equilibrium model](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3094757) (DIEM) has been doing fairly well for awhile — coming up on [two years](https://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2018/02/dynamic-equilibrium-in-wage-growth.html) in a couple months!



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium wage growth 0.png>)

J.W. Mason [will have to continue to be puzzled](https://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2019/08/nimbyism-in-labor-market-or-static.html). Black is post-forecast data, and click to enlarge. We can also say the DIEM forecast was better than Jan Hatzius' (Goldman Sachs) forecast from this same week [one year ago](https://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2018/11/ill-say-similar-things-for-half-salary.html). Orange is the actual average over that time period with one standard deviation errors, compared with Hatzius' range in purple:



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium wage growth.png>)

Other forecasts where the DIEM is outperforming are RGDP growth and PCE inflation (those belated GDP updates). These cases aren't so much about the center of the prediction, but rather the error band being smaller for the DIEM model and still being accurate.



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -PCE inflation- DATA UPDATES compare FRB NY -plus new FRB NY-.png>)![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -ngdp deflator rgdp- FRB model -data updates-.png>)

There's also the FOMC forecast, which is fine but claims a lot more precision in their "central tendency" that must be reflecting something other than RMS error \[1\]:



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -ngdp deflator rgdp- FOMC forecastsl -data updates-.png>)

And here's [the forecast](https://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2017/03/the-quantity-theory-of-labor-and.html) of nominal GDP (NGDP) over employment ([PAYEMS](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/PAYEMS) or here _L_) that forms the basis of (the information equilibrium view of) Okun's law and the "[quantity theory of labor](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2016/03/a-quantity-theory-of-labor-and-capital.html)":
