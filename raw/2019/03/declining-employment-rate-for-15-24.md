I can't seem to find the original forecast (maybe I never published it?), but I did look at the [employment rate for 15-24 year olds](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/LREM24TTUSM156S) with the [dynamic information equilibrium model](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3094757) some time in late 2017. In any case, it's not the forecast that's interesting but rather the deviation from it:



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -emp rate 15-24- data updates 2.png>)

This metric is showing some deviations that could be indicative of a potential recession in the 2020 time frame (in line with other indicators [such as the yield curve](https://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2019/03/market-updates-2k19-and-old-forecast.html)). However, it also showed a false positive in the mid-90s:



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -emp rate 15-24- data updates.png>)

The [unemployment rate in the same age group](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/LRUN24TTUSM156S) might help explain the false positive as we can see a dramatic dip in the rate at the same time indicating that people 15-24 were leaving the labor force (potentially going to college through [newly expanded student loan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Direct_Student_Loan_Program) programs).



![](<media/dynamic equilibrium -unemployment 15-24- data updates.png>)

However the more recent downturn in the employment rate doesn't have an immediate corollary in the unemployment rate.



In any case, it will be useful to watch these employment metrics.
