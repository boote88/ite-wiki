Today I analyzed the EU with the information transfer model and produced the analogous graphs from [this post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/07/dotting-is-and-crossing-ts.html) (the price level), [this post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/08/the-interest-rate-in-information.html) (interest rates) and [this post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/the-liquidity-trap-and-information-trap.html) (interest rates, the liquidity trap and the information trap). The monetary base data came from [here](http://sdw.ecb.europa.eu/quickview.do?SERIES_KEY=123.ILM.M.U2.C.LT01.Z5.EUR) and the GDP, GDP deflator and interest rate data came from [FRED](http://research.stlouisfed.org/fred2/tags/series?t=euro+area). Altogether the story is similar to the US and Japan. The EU is in an information/liquidity trap. The interesting result is that this tests [the hypothesis](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/the-liquidity-trap-and-information-trap.html) that the zero lower bound is not a necessary requirement to be in the trap. Scott Sumner [frequently points out](http://www.themoneyillusion.com/?p=21621) that the ECB is not at the zero lower bound (rates are at ~1%) as an argument against Paul Krugman and the liquidity trap. **However, this analysis shows that the "liquidity trap" interest rate is almost 2% in the EU** ([in Japan](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/japan-interest-rates-and-liquidity-trap.html) it is 1% and [in the US](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/09/the-liquidity-trap-and-information-trap.html) it is 0.1%).
