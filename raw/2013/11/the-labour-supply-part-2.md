In the [previous post](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/11/the-labour-supply-part-1.html), I noted that the deviation of the empirical data from the model [P:NGDP→LS](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/08/scott-sumners-model-part-2_30.html) becomes large over [the long time series from the BOE](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/11/the-long-run-in-uk.html). I also noticed that it seems like this deviation prevented us from defining a "natural rate" of unemployment in the model [P:NGDP→U](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/10/the-phillips-curve.html) unless we account for the effect. So starting from [P:NGDP→LS](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/08/scott-sumners-model-part-2_30.html) for the US we have this fit to the price level:

[P:NGDP→U](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/10/the-phillips-curve.html)





![](<media/wage stickiness -comparing with uk and looking at nairu- 3.png>)

If we then use the price level (green curve) to represent the "natural rate" of unemployment (i.e. what the blue curve $\sim NGDP/U$ should be), we can show this plot of the unemployment rate (black) and the "natural rate" (blue dashed):



![](<media/wage stickiness -comparing with uk and looking at nairu- 4.png>)




where $L$ is the civilian labor force and $LS$ is the total number of people employed. The latter term is effectively the "employment rate" and is typically $\simeq 1$.



We can do a similar manipulation for the UK data resulting this fit of the model [P:NGDP→U](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/10/the-phillips-curve.html) to the price level:



![](<media/uk long run nairu 1.png>)

Which is an improvement over the [naive result](http://informationtransfereconomics.blogspot.com/2013/11/the-labour-supply-part-1.html) here. The natural rate can then be derived using the green in the previous graph as we did for the US data above:



![](<media/uk long run nairu 2.png>)

This result has a remarkably constant "natural rate". Too remarkable -- I imagine the data for the total number of employed, the total number of unemployed and the unemployment rate are all based off of the same measure. However, the important result is that $u^{*} \simeq \kappa_{LS}/\kappa_{U}$.
