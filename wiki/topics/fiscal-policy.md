# Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy occupies a special position in the [[information-transfer-model]]: it is one of the few tools that works **inside the [[information-trap]]**, precisely because it doesn't rely on the monetary transmission mechanism.

## Why fiscal policy works

The economy consists of many markets in a distribution of growth states, maintained by [[entropic-forces]]. Government is a single large entity (~10–30% of GDP) that can move by political fiat — it is not subject to the entropic forces holding the rest of the distribution in place. A fiscal expansion shifts the entire distribution's mean rightward.

**Crowding out** would require the rest of the economy to spontaneously coordinate toward lower growth to offset the government's expansion. This is a spontaneous entropy decrease — thermodynamically absurd. Ricardian equivalence, in this picture, is "the silly argument that other molecules decide to get colder out of spite."

The **Keynesian multiplier** is the rest of the economy re-thermalizing to the new average — like molecules reaching thermal equilibrium after energy is injected via microwave heating of water molecules.

## Why it works in the information trap

In the [[information-trap]], $\partial P/\partial \text{MB} \approx 0$ — monetary policy can't move the needle. But $\partial P/\partial \text{NGDP} \neq 0$ — changes in output still affect prices normally. This means fiscal stimulus (which directly adds to NGDP) cannot be "offset" by monetary policy, because monetary policy is impotent. The monetarist objection — that the central bank will sterilize fiscal expansion — fails when the economy is trapped.

## ARRA counterfactual analysis

Using the ITM for counterfactual analysis of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act:
- ARRA reduced the unemployment peak from ~13% to 10% (a 2.5 percentage point effect)
- Fiscal multiplier ≈ 1.25, consistent with CBO/IMF estimates
- Monetary offset of fiscal policy was minimal (~2%) — the Fed couldn't sterilize fiscal expansion near the information trap

NGDP shocks extracted by the ITM correlate with Robert Waldmann's "paleo-Keynesian" metric for government stimulus, both with and without a multiplier — even the simplest Keynesian model outperforms a monetary offset model (which would predict zero fiscal impact).
