# Secular Stagnation

Larry Summers' "secular stagnation" hypothesis — that developed economies face a persistent shortfall in demand and investment — is reinterpreted in the [[information-transfer-model]] not as a mysterious new condition but as a **structural feature** of mature economies that the framework predicted from first principles. The explanation has deepened over time from a purely monetary story (κ rising toward 1) to a broader entropic and demographic picture.

## The κ / temperature story

In the original ITM framing, secular stagnation is simply the economy approaching the [[information-trap]]. As κ rises toward 1, the price level becomes insensitive to monetary changes. Low inflation and low growth are the natural state of a large economy — not a failure of policy but a consequence of the mathematics. The [[economic-potentials]] framework makes this precise: a larger economy is a **colder economy** ($T \sim 1/\kappa$). High-κ economies have more configurations made up of many low-growth markets than a few high-growth ones, so the maximum-entropy state is low-growth.

## The demographic story

The [[quantity-theory-of-labor]] adds a deeper layer. If inflation is driven by labor force growth (~2.67× the CLF growth rate), then "secular stagnation" is just **slow population growth**. The post-war growth miracle was partly inflated by a demographic transition (women and minorities entering the workforce), a government spending transition (from ~0% to ~21% of GDP), and a consumer credit transition. All three were logistic processes that have now largely completed.

Once you subtract these transitions from the data, the "new normal" of slower growth looks like it may be the **first genuinely normal period** in the post-war record. Every prior recovery was boosted by at least one major structural transition. The post-2008 recovery, derided as the "weakest in American history," may simply be the first one not inflated by such a transition. See [[recessions]] for the full argument.

## The partition function story

The partition function approach in the [[economic-potentials]] framework makes the stagnation mechanism even more precise. The Gibbs measure says the probability of occupying a high-$k$ state decreases exponentially with both $k$ and the size of the factor of production (money or labor). As economies grow, the maximum-entropy distribution shifts toward low-growth states. This isn't a policy failure; it's a counting argument. More firms appearing faster than the factor of production grows pushes κ down further, accelerating the trend.

The laser-vs-blackbody framing: mainstream growth theory assumes a "laser" economy (every market growing at the same rate). The reality is a "blackbody" (an ensemble of markets in different growth states). The average growth rate falls as the economy grows because the Boltzmann distribution increasingly favors low-growth states.

## Evidence

- **[[ecb-eurozone|EU]]**: predicted to deflate in November 2013, confirmed in 2015. Structural κ near the trap. ECB QE produced no change.
- **[[japan]]**: decades of stagnation after crossing the κ = 1 ridge. Demographics (shrinking labor force) make the situation even more structural.
- **US**: approaching the trap boundary. The [[quantity-theory-of-labor]] explains why — population growth below 0.75%/year makes a 2% inflation target structurally unattainable.
- **Canada**: undershooting its inflation target exactly as predicted since 2014, with zero weight on central bank actions.

## Why "secular stagnation" is misleading

The framing suggests something is *wrong* — that the economy should be growing faster and something is preventing it. The ITM says nothing is wrong. Growth and inflation in developed economies are approaching their maximum-entropy levels. There is no "cure" because there is no disease. Accelerating demographics (through immigration, for example) would raise the equilibrium growth rate; no amount of monetary or fiscal engineering will produce a sustained change in the absence of demographic changes.

The Summers/Krugman story (insufficient demand, secular shortfall in investment) mistakes the destination for a trap. The economy isn't stuck below potential; the potential has shifted.
