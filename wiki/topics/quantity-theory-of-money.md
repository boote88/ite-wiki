# Quantity Theory of Money (QTM) in the ITM Framework

The [[information-transfer-model]] subsumes the Quantity Theory of Money as a special case.

## QTM limitations

Pure QTM predicts a 1:1 relationship between monetary base growth and inflation. This works reasonably at high inflation but **breaks down at low inflation** — the relationship becomes noisy and unreliable.

Standard economics assumes doubling the monetary base halves cash value (linear 1/x relationship). The ITM predicts a **logarithmic** relationship instead: doubling MB reduces value to ~$1/e \approx 0.37$, not 0.5, because doubling the "bits" available follows information-theoretic (logarithmic) scaling.

## ITM + QTM

Testing against cross-country data (from Barro textbook) on inflation vs. monetary base growth:
- ITM + QTM performs as well or better than pure QTM across all inflation levels
- Crucially, ITM + QTM works at **low inflation** where QTM alone fails
- QTM is effectively a limiting case of the ITM framework

## Implications

The ITM provides the missing piece that makes the quantity theory work at low inflation: the [[information-transfer-index]] κ captures the non-linear relationship between money supply and prices that QTM assumes away.

## Monetarism as a degenerative research program

Modern monetarism has the structure of what Lakatos called a "degenerative research program" — its central claim (money causes inflation) keeps surviving by redefining "money." First it was gold; then M1; when M1 didn't track inflation, M2; when M2 didn't, M3; when QE massively expanded the monetary base without producing inflation, it shifted to *expectations* of monetary expansion; and when expectations didn't hold, *expectations of monetary expansion that might be taken away.* Each redefinition saves the appearance of the theory at the cost of measuring something more abstract and less observable.

George Selgin's monetarist primer is a clean illustration: there's supply and demand for money, but no real way to measure either, so we should use the concept of money demand (which is different from wanting money) and define economic growth as money demand growing slower than the unmeasurable money supply. As Smith summarizes: "We don't need to decide what money is because we just assume how macroeconomics works." The information equilibrium framework makes this kind of move impossible — every relationship has to specify what variables are in equilibrium with what, and those variables have to be measurable. The ITM's answer to "what is money?" — empirically, it's M0 (currency in circulation) for the price level and MB for short-term interest rates (see [[currency-vs-reserves]]) — was found by checking which aggregate actually fit the data, not by retreating to ever-more-abstract definitions.
