# Quantitative Easing

QE — central banks purchasing financial assets to expand the monetary base — is one of the ITM's strongest empirical validations. The model predicted QE would fail to produce inflation, and it was right.

## Why QE doesn't cause inflation

The answer comes from [[currency-vs-reserves]]: QE expands **reserves** (the MB − M0 component of the monetary base) but barely affects **currency in circulation** (M0). Since the price level is determined by P:NGDP→M0, and M0 barely moved, inflation didn't respond. Post-2008 data shows reserves are **completely uncorrelated** with inflation — arguably the cleanest natural experiment in macroeconomics.

QE does affect **short-term interest rates** (determined by r:NGDP→MB), which is why rates fell. But rates and inflation are governed by different aggregates.

## The "cleanest experiment"

Central bank reserves (MB − M0) have been completely uncorrelated with inflation since 2008. Only M0 remains correlated with price level changes. This is not a model prediction that could be debated — it's a direct empirical observation. The ITM predicted this separation; mainstream models that treat "the money supply" as a single aggregate did not.

## Cross-country evidence

- **US**: Fed's QE was insufficient to prevent deflation risk, but also couldn't cause inflation. The model suggests QE needed to be roughly twice as large to prevent deflation — but even then, it would have affected rates, not prices.
- **ECB**: the trillion-euro QE announced in January 2015 produced **no change** to ITM inflation predictions. "The QE; it does nothing!"
- **Japan**: decades of monetary base expansion produced no inflation because M0 barely grew and the economy had crossed the [[information-trap]] ridge.

## Implications

QE is not useless — it affects short-term rates and provides liquidity. But it cannot generate inflation because it expands the wrong aggregate. The [[information-trap]] makes this especially clear: when κ → 1, the price level is insensitive to monetary base changes regardless of how large they are.
