# The Paradox of Fiat Money

Why does fiat money have value? Standard theory says because it's expected to have value — but this creates a "greater sucker" problem: rational people only accept money because they expect to find someone else to accept it before its value vanishes. Since the world will eventually end, backward induction says expected value should be zero today. The [[information-transfer-model]] inverts this argument.

## The ITM resolution: you're the sucker if you refuse

With $n \gg 1$ goods and services in the economy, a maximum entropy argument shows the most likely state of the economy **saturates the budget constraint** — all money is in use across many transactions. This maximizes the ideality of information transfer, and therefore **maximizes prices** for a given level of goods and services. The key equation:

$$\frac{k N}{M} \geq \frac{dN}{dM} \equiv P$$

Whoever trades goods for the most widely used money gets a **higher price** (more ideal information transfer) than someone using barter or credit (very non-ideal transfer). If you refuse to accept money, you're the greater sucker — you get a worse deal and contribute to your own wealth's decline.

This critically depends on $n \gg 1$: money's value comes from being exchangeable for as many things as possible. A currency accepted by 10,000 merchants is more valuable to hold than one accepted by 10, because the former provides access to more of state space.

## Money as dissipative structure

In the [[entropic-forces]] framework, money is a **dissipative structure** — like convection cells in heated water or even life itself. It arises to maximize entropy production, moving the system toward equilibrium. Convection cells cease when water reaches uniform temperature; money loses value only when every scarce resource is equally allocated (the economic equivalent of heat death).

The rational agent explanation (it's in my self-interest to accept money) may be like [[sticky-prices|Calvo pricing]] or incentives: an attempt to microfound a force that only exists at the macro level. Money may have no true microeconomic explanation — it's an emergent structure whose effects are [[entropic-forces]].

## Money defined via Noether's theorem

The deepest definition: **money is that which is conserved because of the homogeneity of degree zero of the economic universe.** If you double both supply and demand, prices stay the same — that scale invariance (homogeneity of degree zero) implies a conserved quantity via Noether's theorem, and we call that conserved quantity money. This parallels energy (conserved because of time-translation symmetry) and momentum (conserved because of space-translation symmetry).

Anything that becomes an intermediary between supply and demand can be money as long as it maintains [[information-equilibrium]] — changes in one side are faithfully transmitted through the intermediary to the other. Tally marks, gold, fiat currency all work. The most effective money is something usable for many products, without intrinsic value, and completely fungible (high entropy). The formal definition:

*Money is a thing that mediates transactions and has high information entropy.*

## Money is not points

A common confusion (especially among Austrian economists and goldbugs): money should reflect intrinsic value, and fiat currency is sinful because anyone can produce it cheaply. The pinball analogy clarifies what money actually does: in the days of electromechanical pinball, points required actual labor (a wheel had to physically turn). Modern digital pinball produced massive "point inflation" because the points are just numbers. Yet pinball points still rank-order players on the same machine.

Money isn't even points. Points rank-order labor on the same machine; money does not rank-order labor across the economy. A Walmart executive making the same as a doctor isn't doing equivalent good in any objective sense. A US doctor doesn't do meaningfully more good than a doctor in any other country, despite earning vastly more. Money isn't a measure of value or contribution — it's a particular **solution to the allocation problem**, which the market finds via maximum entropy. Whether that solution is "optimal" by any objective function is unknowable (the linear programming problem is intractable), and the maximum-entropy allocation tends to follow Pareto distributions because of the constraint on $\langle \log x \rangle$ — with results that make most empirical income distributions look more like physics than ethics.

This is why labor *can* be translated into fiat currency without any intrinsic value: the currency stands for a particular allocation, not for the worth of the labor. And it's why we can live in a society where the supply of currency is manipulated to mitigate macroeconomic fluctuations — the "value" of the currency was always a maximum-entropy convention, never a measurement.
