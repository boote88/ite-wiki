# Cross-Country Comparisons

The [[information-transfer-index]] κ serves as a universal descriptor for comparing monetary policy effectiveness across countries. Smith developed a normalized **κ-σ space** where different economies can be plotted on the same graph, revealing that they all sit on the same underlying price surface — their different macro outcomes arise from different positions on that surface, not from different economic structures.

## Country profiles

| Country | κ regime | Monetary policy | Key observations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Australia | Low κ | Highly effective | Escaped 2008 recession; monetary policy has traction |
| Sweden | κ ≈ 0.67 | Pure QTM; effective | Near-constant κ since 1980s; escaped traps in 1990s and 2008 |
| Canada | Low-moderate, rising | Effective but approaching limit | Predicted to undershoot inflation target — confirmed by mid-2015 |
| US | κ rising toward 1 | Approaching trap | Post-2008 QE insufficient; oscillating M0 growth shows Fed struggling |
| UK | Moderate-high κ | Partially effective | 140+ years of BoE data validated; WWII phase transition visible |
| Switzerland | High κ | Trapped | Pegged franc to Euro, still got deflation; abandoned peg Jan 2015 |
| [[ecb-eurozone\|EU]] | κ near trap | Trapped (not at ZLB!) | Deflation at 1-2% rates; ECB's trillion-euro QE produced no change |
| [[japan]] | κ > 1 | Trapped; expansion counterproductive | Crossed ridge ~2000; 3.5x MB expansion worsened situation |

## The key insight

Countries can't easily change their structural κ. Their position on the price surface moves over time as MB and NGDP evolve. The same monetary policy produces different outcomes depending on where a country sits relative to the [[information-trap]] ridge ($\partial P/\partial \text{MB} = 0$). Swapping central bankers between Japan and Australia wouldn't change outcomes — the constraints are structural, not institutional.

## Predictive track record

The cross-country framework doesn't just explain history — it makes predictions that have been confirmed:

**Canada's inflation undershooting**: In mid-2014, Smith predicted Canada would begin undershooting its inflation target by an increasing amount. Canada hadn't been undershooting at the time. By mid-2015, early signs appeared; by early 2017, CPI was well below the 2% target trend with the monetary base right on its log-linear trend. This was predicted with **zero weight on any central bank actions** — the undershooting is structural (more ways for a complex economy to be in low-growth states than high-growth ones). As Smith noted: "This success is also a major failure of most other approaches from theories where central banks set expectations, to quantity theories, to Taylor rules. This undershooting was predictable with zero weight placed on any of the actions of the central bank."

![Canada CPI undershooting trend in 2017](<../media/2017-01/canada undershoot cpi.png>)

**Eurozone deflation**: Predicted in November 2013 via the information trap analysis. Confirmed in early 2015 with the EU entering outright deflation.

**Switzerland's failed depreciation**: The SNB pegged the franc to the Euro to produce inflation. It didn't work — [[exchange-rates]] decouple from [[inflation]] in the information trap. The SNB abandoned the peg in January 2015, exactly as the model predicted would be necessary.

**Japan's Abenomics**: The brief rise in inflation attributed to Abe's policies was mean reversion from the largest negative deviation in the model, not a policy success. Inflation returned to the deflationary trend.

## Historical validation

- **UK**: 140+ years of Bank of England data validated against ITM with a single fit across monetary regimes, including the WWII phase transition
- **US 1798–present**: 200+ years of data show two distinct κ regimes with a phase transition at WWII
- **The 1970s**: cross-country data shows the 1970s were not anomalous — the path through MB-NGDP space was continuous. Oil shocks were minor perturbations. Countries experienced different outcomes based on their κ position, not their policies
