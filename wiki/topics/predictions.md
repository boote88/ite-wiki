# Predictions and Forecasting Track Record

A central feature of the ITM research program is [[jason-smith]]'s commitment to making quantitative, falsifiable predictions and tracking them publicly over multi-year horizons. He maintained a running "prediction aggregation" page and regularly compared his forecasts against institutional models.

## Methodology

Smith's approach to prediction is distinctive:

- **Ex-ante forecasts**: predictions made and published before data arrives, not backtests
- **Multi-year tracking**: forecasts tracked over 2–5 year horizons with regular updates
- **Apples-to-apples comparisons**: uses vintage data (ALFRED) to compare against forecasts made at the same time
- **Parameter-count accounting**: always reports how many parameters his model uses vs competitors
- **Public accountability**: maintains prediction archives with smiley/sad face outcomes
- **Out-of-sample tests**: fits model to progressively longer data windows (1960–70, 1960–75, etc.) to test prediction stability. A fit to 1960–70 data alone predicts low inflation in the 2000s

## The "five parameters, six variables" framework

The centerpiece quantitative forecast, first made March 2014: a single ITM model with 5 fitted parameters simultaneously tracks:

1. Interest rates (3-month and 10-year)
2. Core CPI inflation
3. Unemployment rate
4. RGDP growth
5. Price level
6. NGDP shocks

**Sources:** [Macroeconomic predictions for 2016 (2014-03)](../raw/2014/03/macroeconomic-predictions-for-2016.html), [Not bad for five parameters, take two (2015-01-08)](../raw/2015/01/not-bad-for-five-parameters-take-two.md)

**Outcome:** Declared successful in [April 2016 retrospective](../raw/2016/04/celebrate-this-blogs-birthday-with.md) after tracking for two years. 2017 update confirmed success.

### ITM vs Federal Reserve Board

"Jason versus the Fed" series, started September 2014. Compares ITM predictions against the FRB/US model (~60 stochastic equations, 320 identities, 125 exogenous variables).

**Outcome:** ITM outperforming consistently through January 2015 and beyond. First few measurements jumped outside Fed's 90% confidence interval.

**Sources:** [Jason versus the Fed (2014-09)](../raw/2014/09/jason-versus-fed.md), [[2015-01-14-better-than-fed]], [Comparing to Fed forecasts from 2014 (2018-10)](../raw/2018/10/comparing-to-fed-forecasts-from-2014.md)

### ITM vs TIPS spreads

TIPS spread as inflation predictor compared to ITM.

- ITM average error (2007–2014 Q1): −12 basis points
- TIPS average error: +66 basis points
- ITM ~5x more accurate

The 2007 ITM prediction was better than TIPS predictions starting from almost any date.

**Source:** [Better than TIPS (2014-07)](../raw/2014/07/better-than-tips.md)

### ITM vs Smets-Wouters DSGE, BVAR, and Fed Greenbook

Systematic apples-to-apples comparison over 1992–2006 period. ITM was "top dog in the average case" despite having 9 parameters vs 36 for DSGE.

## Cross-country predictions

### Eurozone deflation

- **Predicted:** November 2013, from [[information-trap]] framework
- **Validated:** Early 2015 — Eurozone entered deflation
- **Source:** [[2015-01-08-eurozone-deflation]]

ECB's trillion-euro QE announcement (January 2015) produced no change to ITM inflation predictions. "The QE; it does nothing!" See [[quantitative-easing]].

### Canada inflation undershooting

- **Predicted:** Mid-2014 — Canada would undershoot its inflation target by increasing amounts (Canada was not undershooting at the time)
- **Validated:** Mid-2015 — confirmed; inflation systematically fell below target as κ increased
- **Source:** [Worthwhile Canadian prediction (2014-07)](../raw/2014/07/worthwhile-canadian-prediction.md), [500 posts celebration (2015-06)](../raw/2015/06/celebrating-500-posts-with-predictive.md)

Canada exchange rate forecast (beginning 2016) also validated by Q3 2016 — [Another IT model success (2016-12)](../raw/2016/12/another-it-model-success-forecasting.md).

### Switzerland: currency depreciation fails

- **Predicted:** August 2014 — SNB's currency peg would fail to produce inflation
- **Validated:** January 15, 2015 — SNB abandoned peg after devaluation failed
- **Further validated:** February 2016 — core HICP showed predicted continuation of trend, not the deflation standard monetary theory predicted
- **Sources:** [[2015-01-15-switzerland-deflation]], [Another win for IT model: Switzerland (2016-02)](../raw/2016/02/another-win-for-it-model-switzerland.md)

### Japan

- **Original model:** 2013, fitted 2005–2013 data
- **Correction:** January 12, 2015 — discovered fitting error. Full-period fit (1994–2013) revealed Abenomics inflation was mean reversion from the largest negative deviation, not a policy success
- **Subsequent tracking:** Model predictions continued to track actual data through July 2015 and into 2016
- **Sources:** [[2015-01-12-japan-correction]], [Model prediction holding up for Japan (2015-07)](../raw/2015/07/model-prediction-holding-up-for-japan.md)

### UK

- **Tested:** 140+ years of Bank of England data
- **Outcome:** Single ITM fit across multiple monetary regimes, including visible WWII phase transition
- **Source:** [[cross-country-comparisons]]

### China

- **Predicted:** 2014 — sustainable growth of 8%+ through 2020
- **Source:** [[2014-h2-entropy-and-universality]]

## Theoretical / structural predictions

These are predictions about what *must* happen given the framework, without specific dates:

- **NGDP and inflation targeting eventually fail** — maintaining targets requires exponentially increasing M0 growth as κ → 1. See [[2015-01-15-ngdp-targeting-fails]]
- **QE doesn't cause inflation** — reserves ≠ currency. See [[quantitative-easing]], [[currency-vs-reserves]]
- **Fiscal policy works in the [[information-trap]]** — monetary offset impossible when ∂P/∂MB ≈ 0. See [[fiscal-policy]]
- **ARRA reduced unemployment peak** from ~13% to 10% (multiplier ≈ 1.25, consistent with CBO/IMF). Monetary offset was minimal (~2%)
- **κ solves the identification problem** — one parameter determines both S&D elasticities. See [[2015-01-16-identification-problem]]

## Corrections and acknowledged limitations

- **Japan model (Jan 2015):** Fitting error discovered; full-period refit changed interpretation of Abenomics
- **Monetary IE model (mid-2017):** Showed signs of poor performance, possibly approaching failure; Smith notes it may need a "sad face" in the forecast archive
- **Inflation medium-term lower bound:** Smith claims ITM may be unbeatable at 5–15 year inflation prediction, but acknowledges short-term (1–2 quarter) forecasts have high irreducible measurement error
- **Constant inflation benchmark:** Notes that a constant inflation model is hard to beat in 21st century data — some of ITM's advantage is in being right about the *level* rather than the *dynamics*

## Methodological evolution

The forecasting framework evolved over the blog's lifetime:

1. **2013–2015:** Monetary information transfer model — fits P(NGDP, M0) surface with κ
2. **2015–2016:** Information equilibrium (IE) model — more general framework with equivalence relations
3. **2017–2018:** Dynamic Information Equilibrium Model (DIEM) — simplified model focused on dynamic equilibrium paths and shocks, applied to unemployment, CPI, S&P 500, and other series
