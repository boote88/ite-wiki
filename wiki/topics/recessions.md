# Recessions

The [[information-transfer-model]] gives recessions a unified treatment that doesn't fit cleanly into either the standard "demand shock" or "supply shock" frames. Recessions are **coordination failures** — moments when humans (uniquely among physical systems) spontaneously coordinate their behavior, reducing the entropy of the economic state and producing large negative [[nominal-shocks]]. They're aperiodic avalanches, not periodic cycles, and the dynamics borrow more from self-organized criticality than from any standard business-cycle theory.

## What recessions are in the ITM

A recession is a period of large [[non-ideal-information-transfer]] — the system has departed from the efficiency bound and is operating well below its potential. In the [[plucking-model]] framing, recessions are downward "plucks" away from the [[information-equilibrium]] ceiling; in the [[entropic-forces]] framing, they're spontaneous decreases in macroeconomic entropy.

The mechanism is uniquely human: atoms can't coordinate to produce a spontaneous entropy decrease (the second law of thermodynamics forbids it), but humans can. Mass pessimism, panic, herd behavior — these temporarily correlate the behavior of many agents, narrowing the distribution of macroeconomic states from "spread out across many possibilities" to "everyone bunched in a few low-output possibilities." The aggregate consequence is a fall in NGDP, a rise in unemployment, and the constellation of effects we call a recession. The traffic-jam analogy is exact: vehicles slow in coordination, throughput collapses, and the jam dissolves only when coordination breaks down again.

## The fluctuation theorem and the size distribution

This raises an obvious question: if recessions are violations of the "second law of econo-dynamics," why don't they happen all the time? The answer comes from the **fluctuation theorem** of statistical physics, which provides a quantitative bound on entropy-decrease events: the probability of a violation of size $\Delta S$ falls exponentially with the size of the system. For systems as large as a national economy ($\sim 10^7$ active agents and many more transactions), large coordinated entropy decreases are exponentially rare — but not impossible.

This matches the empirical distribution of recessions. They're rare relative to expansions, but they do happen. The size distribution has a long tail: occasional small slowdowns plus rare large catastrophes. **Internal devaluations** (large coordinated wage cuts in places like Latvia or Greece) are even rarer than ordinary recessions and take longer in larger economies — exactly as the fluctuation theorem predicts for systems with more agents.

## Aperiodic avalanches, not cycles

Smith repeatedly distinguishes the ITM picture from "limit cycle" / periodic models of the business cycle (Steve Keen and others). In a limit cycle model, recessions recur on something like a clock — the system oscillates around an attractor. In the ITM, recessions are **aperiodic avalanches** generated by something closer to self-organized criticality: the monetary base is a sand pile that grows over time, accumulating potential for departure from equilibrium. Most additions to the pile are absorbed quietly. Occasionally, a small disturbance triggers an avalanche of arbitrary size.

The size distribution should follow a power law (more small recessions than large), and recessions should be hard to predict in advance — both empirically true. There is no clock, no "cycle." The 1990s expansion lasted a decade not because the cycle "wasn't ready" but because no avalanche happened to trigger.

The hazard function is consistent with this. Fitting the empirical distribution of post-WWII recovery lengths to a gamma distribution yields a mean of 4.8 years and a standard deviation of 3.1 years — so recoveries do "grow old" (the SF Fed's claim that they don't is an artifact of using a graph window that hides the leveling-off), but the variance is so wide that the hazard function looks nearly flat over most of the recovery's life. Pre-WWII the mean was 2.2 years with σ ≈ 1 year — much sharper. The post-WWII spread is much larger, consistent with avalanches triggered by largely random coordination events rather than cyclical clockwork.

## The Great Recession: a complete causal timeline

The ITM's story of the 2008 crisis evolved over time. The early version (2014) attributed it to Fed tightening from 2005 onward. By 2018, the [[dynamic-information-equilibrium]] seismogram provides a much richer and more precise timeline:

![Great Recession seismogram — complete causal chain](<../media/2018-11/historyGR2.png>)

**Mid-2006**: The very first signal is a drop in **construction hires** — before housing starts decline, before the Fed stops raising rates, and over a year before the NBER recession. Jobs were still being advertised in construction, but fewer were being hired, suggesting a labor supply constraint rather than a demand collapse.

**Late 2006–2007**: Drop in **conceptions** (a social indicator preceding economic indicators), Fed conference calls about collapsing housing markets, first rate cut. The Case-Shiller index begins its negative shock.

**2008**: The events the media and most economists focus on — Lehman collapse, stock market crash, GDP decline, unemployment spike — all come **in the middle or at the end** of a process that started two years earlier.

**Debt**: Negative shocks to debt measures come *after* the recession is underway. Debt may have worsened the recession's severity but did not determine its timing.

**A speculative cause: xenophobia.** Anti-immigrant legislation and protests erupted in March 2006, exactly when construction hires began falling. E-verify mandates prevented employers from hiring undocumented workers. Immigration from Mexico had been declining since 2004. The construction sector — heavily dependent on immigrant labor — experienced a labor supply shock that cascaded through the economy. This would make the Great Recession partly a self-inflicted wound from anti-immigrant policy, not purely a financial crisis.

The earlier monetary narrative (NGDP-M0 path pushed against trend by Fed tightening) remains valid as a complementary view, but the DIEM seismogram shows a more complex multi-year process starting with a labor supply shock in construction, not a single monetary event.

The post-crisis QE response was much larger than necessary in the ITM's accounting. A roughly $200 billion expansion would have sufficed; the Fed expanded by ~$3 trillion. The extra reserves had no effect on inflation (see [[currency-vs-reserves]] and [[quantitative-easing]]).

## Recovery regularity

A surprising empirical observation: post-recession employment recoveries follow remarkably similar trajectories across decades. The 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s recoveries all look about the same when overlaid, despite very different policy responses, very different underlying causes, and very different productive structures. This is hard to reconcile with stories that attribute recessions to specific micro mechanisms (search costs, technology shocks, financial frictions) — those mechanisms vary across decades, but the recovery shape doesn't.

In the ITM, this regularity is mechanical. Recovery is the system thermalizing back toward maximum entropy after a coordination failure. The destination — the [[plucking-model|efficiency ceiling]] — is the same across decades. Only the magnitude and timing of the initial pluck vary. The journey looks similar because it's the same physical process every time. See [[labor-market]] for the same point applied specifically to employment dynamics.

Quantitatively, the unemployment rate falls during recoveries at a remarkably constant rate of about **0.49 percentage points per year** (~0.04 pp/month). This is the closest thing in macroeconomics to an "unemployment equilibrium" — but it's an equilibrium in the *slope*, not the *level*. The mainstream concept of NAIRU as a stable level (5%, 25%, whatever the DSGE model implies) doesn't match the data: unemployment never sits at any particular level for more than a year or two. What's stable is the *recovery rate*. Once you slope-correct the data by adding $\alpha t$, recoveries become approximately horizontal lines and recessions become more vertical transitions. The Volcker recessions of the 1980s look anomalous in this view — possibly because they were Fed-induced rather than spontaneous avalanches — but every other recession in the post-war record fits the pattern.

## No equilibrium data: the post-2008 recovery as "first normal"

A striking observation from the [[dynamic-information-equilibrium]] analysis: virtually **all quality post-war macro data comes from periods affected by major structural transitions**. The demographic shift of the 1970s (women entering workforce), the government spending transition of the 1940s (from ~0% to ~21% of GDP), and the consumer credit transition of the 1950s each lasted ~20 years. Together they cover almost the entire post-war period. The remaining ~20 years of "normal" data contain the 2008 crisis and two massive asset bubbles.

The implication: if anyone claims to know what "normal" macroeconomic growth looks like, they're comparing to data that was inflated by one or more of these transitions. The post-2008 recovery — the "weakest recovery in American history" per conventional commentary — may actually be the **first recovery not inflated by a major structural transition**. Comparing it unfavorably to prior recoveries that were boosted by demographic or credit transitions is apples to oranges.

## Conceptions as the earliest leading indicator

The [[dynamic-information-equilibrium]] model applied to conception rates reveals them as the **longest-lead indicator** of recessions — conception rates begin falling several quarters before NBER recession dates, preceding even JOLTS hires, job openings, and quits. A social indicator leads all economic indicators, suggesting recessions begin as diffuse shifts in social sentiment (family planning decisions) before manifesting in hiring, spending, and output data.

The DIEM fits conception rate data well, and its shocks in the seismogram consistently precede all purely economic time series. This supports the [[entropic-forces]] interpretation: recessions involve human coordination that starts as shared social pessimism and only later crystallizes into correlated economic behavior. A recession is a complex multi-year process that is already underway in social life long before the NBER calls it.

## Why interest rates aren't a good policy indicator

A consequence of the avalanche framing: trying to "steer" the economy with interest rates is like pouring more snow on a mountain to manage when an avalanche happens. You can postpone the avalanche but you also increase its eventual size. The Fed's mid-2000s tightening was successful in cooling the economy — and successful in setting up a much larger avalanche than would otherwise have occurred. There is no "stance" of monetary policy that is independent of where the economy already sits on the [[information-trap|information surface]] (see [[unobservables]]).

This is why interest rates as a policy indicator are deeply misleading. The trend of "neutral" interest rates moves with the economy's position on the surface, so a 4% rate in 1985 and a 4% rate in 2005 mean entirely different things about how tight or loose policy actually is. The ITM provides the alternative: tight money is when the actual NGDP-M0 path is above its theoretical trend, regardless of what nominal rates happen to be.

## Connection to other concepts

Recessions are where many ITM concepts converge:

- **[[plucking-model]]**: recessions are the plucks below the efficiency ceiling
- **[[entropic-forces]]**: the mechanism is coordination-driven entropy decrease
- **[[non-ideal-information-transfer]]**: the formal framework — recessions are large $I_S < I_D$ events
- **[[nominal-shocks]]**: the empirical handle — recessions show up as large negative shocks
- **[[interest-rates]]**: yield curve inversion as a recession signal has an information-theoretic basis (NGDP-MB path approaching NGDP-M0 trend)
- **[[fiscal-policy]]**: works in recessions because $\partial P/\partial \text{NGDP} \neq 0$ even when monetary policy is impotent
- **[[information-trap]]**: at high κ, the economy becomes more vulnerable to recessions because the bound is flatter and less restoring force pushes it back
