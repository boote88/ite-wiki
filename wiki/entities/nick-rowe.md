# Nick Rowe

Canadian economist at Carleton University, blogging at Worthwhile Canadian Initiative. One of [[jason-smith]]'s most frequent and respected interlocutors — they disagree fundamentally but engage productively.

## Key positions

- Monetary policy works through expectations: if the central bank credibly commits to a target, markets adjust to make it happen
- The "hot potato" mechanism is central to monetary transmission
- Microfoundations are necessary for macro models
- NGDP level targeting is superior to inflation targeting

## ITM engagement

Smith agrees with Rowe on many specifics (NGDP targeting is better than inflation targeting, the hot potato effect is real) while disagreeing on mechanisms. The core dispute: Rowe believes expectations are central and the hot potato effect requires agents to "want" to rebalance portfolios. Smith argues the hot potato effect is [[entropic-forces|thermalization]] — no wanting required — and that [[expectations]] are neither central nor necessary.

Smith coined "people of the concrete steppes" (a play on Rowe's dismissal of those who focus on concrete monetary mechanisms rather than expectations) as a self-description.

On the Walrasian auctioneer, Rowe's collaborator Stephen Williamson inadvertently described [[entropic-forces]] perfectly: microfoundations "would do a pretty poor job at explaining the behaviour of any particular individual, but instead model common tendencies that emerge within large groups."

Rowe has said at various times that he doesn't understand what the ITM blog is about — a frustration Smith attributes to his physics background and writing style rather than the ideas themselves.
