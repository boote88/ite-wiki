# Milton Friedman

Founding monetarist economist. His ideas occupy a complicated position in the ITM: partially vindicated, partially superseded.

## What Friedman got right

- The [[quantity-theory-of-money]] works — but only in the low-κ regime (κ ~ 0.5). When the [[information-transfer-index]] is low, monetary policy is effective and QTM is a good approximation. Friedman was right about the 1970s US because κ was low then.
- The [[plucking-model]] of business cycles — RGDP growth is bounded from above, with recessions as downward deviations from a ceiling. Smith adopted and formalized this within the ITM as temporary [[entropic-forces|entropy reductions]] below the information efficiency bound ($I_S = I_D$).

## What Friedman got wrong

- "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon" — true at high inflation (high monetary base growth) but breaks down at low inflation where the [[information-trap]] operates. Friedman was wrong about Japan and the post-2008 developed world because κ had risen toward 1.
- Friedman noticed that M2 was "truer" than the monetary base — Smith argues he was right to spot the deviation but wrong about the cause. It's the [[information-transfer-index]] changing, not that M2 is a better aggregate.

## The ITM reconciliation

Friedman's monetarism and Keynes' liquidity trap are both correct — in their respective κ regimes. The ITM explains when each applies and provides the smooth transition between them. The history of US economic thought (classical → Keynesian → monetarist → New Keynesian) tracks the trajectory of κ through different regimes.
