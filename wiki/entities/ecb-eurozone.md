# ECB / Eurozone

The European Central Bank and the Eurozone serve as the [[information-transfer-model]]'s "trap caught in real time" — a developed economy that the model called as approaching the [[information-trap]] in November 2013, watched fall into it through 2014, and then watched the ECB attempt every standard tool of escape (QE, currency depreciation, forward guidance) without success. Where [[japan]] is the long-running natural experiment, the EU is the prediction-confirmed-on-camera case.

## The 2013 prediction

In November 2013, when the consensus view treated EU "secular stagnation" as a temporary post-crisis hangover that would resolve itself through normal recovery dynamics, the ITM said the opposite: the Eurozone's [[information-transfer-index]] κ was approaching the trap criterion, and conventional monetary stimulus would not produce inflation. Smith was explicit that the model predicted Eurozone deflation. The prediction was not contingent on any particular ECB action — the structural position of the economy on the price surface was what mattered.

In early 2015 the prediction was confirmed: the Eurozone entered outright deflation, with year-over-year HICP turning negative. By that point the model had been carrying the prediction unchanged for over a year. See [[2015-01-08-eurozone-deflation]] for the immediate update with the data.

## "The QE; it does nothing!"

In January 2015 the ECB announced a roughly trillion-euro QE program. The standard expectation across mainstream macroeconomics was that this would push inflation higher. The ITM made **no change** to its inflation predictions for the rest of 2015 — the monetary base is irrelevant to inflation in the [[currency-vs-reserves|wrong-aggregate]] sense (QE expanded reserves, not currency in circulation), and even if it had reached M0, the high κ would have prevented it from showing up in the price level. The full ITM treatment of QE ineffectiveness is at [[quantitative-easing]].

The ECB's experience is the cleanest natural experiment in QE-and-inflation in the post-2008 period, precisely because the ECB tried it later than the Fed, in a more obviously trapped economy, with predictions on the record from a model that explicitly said it wouldn't work. The result is in: Eurozone inflation did not rebound; the deflation persisted; the QE produced essentially no measurable impact on the price level, exactly as predicted.

## The 500 € note adjustment

A peculiar but illuminating wrinkle in the ITM's Eurozone modeling: high-denomination euro notes (the 500 €) don't behave like ordinary currency. Roughly 9 of 10 of them are used for illegal purposes — cash hoarding, money laundering, illicit transactions — rather than ordinary commerce. They sit in vaults and dark corners, not in the circulation that drives the [[information-equilibrium]] relationship between M0 and the price level.

When 500 € notes are included in M0, the ITM fit to the Eurozone price level is poor. When they're excluded, the fit becomes excellent:

![M0 with vs without 500 € notes — clear improvement when excluded](<../media/2014-09/ite eu 500 euro notes 0.png>)

![ITM fit to Eurozone price level (excluding 500 € notes)](<../media/2014-09/ite eu 500 euro notes 2b.png>)

This is more than a curiosity. It validates the underlying conceptual point: what counts as "money" for the price level is currency that actually mediates transactions, not nominal monetary aggregates. The same principle explains the [[currency-vs-reserves|M0 vs MB]] distinction. Reserves don't circulate; high-denomination euros don't circulate; neither shows up in the price level. The ITM's empirical fit improves dramatically when you exclude what shouldn't be counted.

## Trapped at 1–2% interest rates

A key feature of the EU case: the Eurozone entered the information trap at **non-zero interest rates** — 1% to 2%, well above the [[zero-lower-bound]]. This is what convinced Smith that the [[information-trap]] is genuinely distinct from the textbook liquidity trap, and that Keynes's original definition (which made no reference to zero rates) was closer to the truth than the post-Krugman ZLB framing.

The Eurozone shows that you can get stuck in the trap at any rate level. The defining feature is the slope of the price surface ($\partial P / \partial \text{MB} \approx 0$), not the level of nominal rates. Once κ is high enough, additional monetary action stops moving the price level — the ECB's policy rate could go to 5% or to -1% and the underlying transmission mechanism would still be broken.

## Structural constraints from currency union

A separate but related issue: the Eurozone's structure as a currency union without fiscal union means individual member states cannot use their own monetary policy at all, and ECB policy must serve a heterogeneous group of economies with very different κ values. The "average" κ that determines ECB-wide policy is dominated by the largest economies, which leaves smaller members in even more extreme regimes than the average. Spain's deflation experience in 2014–2015 was significantly worse than the Eurozone average, exactly as you'd expect when an economy with high κ has no monetary policy of its own.

This makes the Eurozone a doubly difficult case: a high-κ regime with no national-level monetary policy and limited fiscal space (the stability pact). The ITM-implied prescription — fiscal expansion, since [[fiscal-policy]] still works in the trap — is institutionally blocked by the EU's design. There is no good policy answer within the existing framework.
