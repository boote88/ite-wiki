# 2014 H2: Entropy and Universality (111 posts)

The second half of 2014 establishes entropy as the ITM's central mechanism, proves universality (applying to traffic, growth models, DSGE), and formalizes expectations as information loss.

## Entropy as central mechanism (Aug–Oct)

### Wage stickiness as entropic force
Nominal wage cuts require *coordinated* information loss comparable to unemployment. Cutting everyone's wages by 5% requires as much coordination as laying off 5% — the entropy cost is equivalent. Hence wages are "sticky" because coordination entropy > individual optimization benefit.

Key visualization: wage distribution entropy loss scenarios comparing unemployment, nominal cuts, and sticky wages — all equivalent entropy costs (raw/2014/10/media/).

### Recessions as coordination failure
Recessions result from **spontaneous human coordination** (mass pessimism/panic) — not external shocks but temporary correlated behavior that reduces entropy and NGDP. Like a traffic jam: individual vehicles slow down in coordination, reducing throughput without any external cause.

Key visualization: uniform distribution coordination illustrations showing entropy loss during recession (raw/2014/10/media/).

### Economic growth = information processing
Nominal growth is unconstrained by physical energy limits — growth is information transactions, not physical throughput. Room for ~10³⁶ factor improvement vs. Boltzmann limit.

## Information equilibrium = ignorance equilibrium (Nov)

The ITM is fundamentally about **maximum ignorance**: assuming the least informative prior about microstates consistent with macro observations. This is not a weakness but matches the thermodynamic precedent — thermodynamics succeeds precisely because it assumes maximum ignorance about molecular states.

Smith argues the ITM **must be correct regardless of the final macro theory**, just as thermodynamics is correct regardless of whether the micro-theory is classical or quantum.

## Inflation as information theory (Dec)

Final synthesis: inflation defined as the gap between theoretical information-carrying capacity of money (M^κ) vs. actual NGDP. Purely information-theoretic, independent of utility, ethics, or agent behavior.
