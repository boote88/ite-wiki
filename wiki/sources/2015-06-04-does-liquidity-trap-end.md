# Does a liquidity trap ever end? (2015-06-04)

[Original post](../../raw/2015/06/does-liquidity-trap-ever-end.md)

A comprehensive synthesis of the [[information-trap]] applied to the "omega point" question: when does monetary policy return to normal? The answer challenges the premise — there may be no normal to return to, and maybe that's fine.

## There is no single natural rate of interest

The equilibrium interest rate is determined by the [[information-equilibrium]] relationship $(r \to p) : \text{NGDP} \to \text{MB}$, giving $\log r = c \log(k \cdot \text{NGDP}/\text{MB})$. This is remarkably effective over 80+ years of US data:

![IE model natural rate (blue) vs data (green)](<../media/2015-06/hyperinflation picture of exit from the Depression 4.png>)

The "natural rate" isn't a constant — it's a moving equilibrium determined by the NGDP/MB ratio. There is no natural rate of inflation either: low inflation is the natural state of a large-base economy and an unnatural state of a small-base one.

## The trap has no well-defined start

Liquidity trap conditions have a **slow onset** — the US started on this path in the 1980s. However, it becomes obvious only when the first shock hits that monetary policy can't handle. The early 2000s recession was light enough to mask the problem; the 2008 shock exposed it. The trap has no microfoundations — it doesn't exist for a single market. It's a macro-level phenomenon tied to [[economic-potentials|economic temperature]] ($\sim 1/\log \text{MB}$). A large monetary base means a cold economy. Temperature doesn't exist for small numbers of atoms; economic temperature doesn't exist for small numbers of markets.

## US history as two regimes

The entire US monetary history can be seen as two regimes: gold standard and fiat currency. The gold standard regime hit its liquidity trap after centuries, producing the Great Depression. The fiat currency regime hit its trap after only ~80 years. The question "does the trap ever end?" is really asking: what broke us out last time?

## Exit mechanisms (expanded)

All historical exits involved some combination of:
- **Redefining the currency** — leaving gold standard for fiat. Can't keep doing this. Electronic money with truly negative rates may be a possibility.
- **Wartime/peacetime hyperinflation** — top-down command economy pushes $\log \text{NGDP}$ up faster than $\log \text{M0}$. Buys decades but you eventually fall back in.
- **Massive Keynesian spending** — pushes NGDP up while keeping M0 growth normal. Same temporary effect.
- **Interest rate peg** — UK appears to have achieved regime change via pegs (twice). Uncertain if it fully exited the trap. Comes with an initial burst of inflation.
- **Loss of central bank independence** — WWII-era Treasury direction of the Fed may have been the mechanism enabling the hyperinflationary exit.

## The provocative question

"Do we really want the liquidity trap to end?" A liquidity-trapped world has low rates and low inflation — retirement insurance is cheaper, infrastructure is cheap to build at 0%. The main cost is losing monetary policy as a lever. But maybe fiscal policy (unemployment insurance, UBI) is a better lever anyway — it doesn't rely on devaluing currency and tricking people with money illusion. Japan has lived with the trap for decades. Maybe we should get used to it.
