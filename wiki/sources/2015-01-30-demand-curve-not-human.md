# Is the demand curve shaped by human behavior? How can we tell? (2015-01-30)

[Original post](../../raw/2015/01/is-demand-curve-shaped-by-human.md)

A provocative argument: the shape of the demand curve has **nothing to do with human behavior** — it emerges from statistics alone, just as pressure emerges from the Maxwell distribution of molecular velocities.

## The classroom experiment debunked

Standard economics classroom experiments ask students to write down reservation prices, then tally how many would buy at each price. As price rises, fewer buyers — looks like a demand curve. But it's actually just an **inverse survival curve of a normal distribution** (a flipped CDF):

## The atom analogy

The same "demand curve" emerges from brainless atoms. Atoms in an ideal gas follow a Maxwell velocity distribution:

![Maxwell velocity distribution](<../media/2015-01/maxwell2.png>)

If atoms "sit down" when velocities higher than theirs are called:

![Atom "demand curve"](<../media/2015-01/maxwell1.png>)

Same shape. No utility maximization, no preferences, no behavior at all.

## Key argument

In the [[information-equilibrium]] framework, supply and demand have literally nothing to do with agent behavior in ideal markets. The mechanics follow from $D = \kappa P S$:
- Hold D constant, S goes up → P falls (demand curve)
- Hold S constant, D goes up → P rises (supply curve)

That's all there is. Students "know" the price of M&M's because it's set by the value of money (economic temperature set by the central bank) — just as atoms "know" the average velocity because it's set by the thermodynamic temperature.

## Significance

This is the strongest statement yet of the [[microfoundations-critique]]: not only are microfoundations unnecessary, they may be invisible in macro data. The demand curve shape is a statistical property of ensembles, not a reflection of utility functions. This connects to [[entropic-forces]] — macro patterns arise from counting, not from individual optimization.
