## Two major cases

### Partial equilibrium → supply and demand diagram

![AB system — supply and demand as information equilibrium](<../media/2015-04/ab system.png>)

One variable adjusts faster than the other. Reconstructs S&D, IS-LM, short-run AD-AS.

### General equilibrium → power laws

Both adjust together: $A \sim B^k$. Gives quantity theory, interest rates, [[solow-growth-model]], Cobb-Douglas functions.

## The philosophical resolution

Markets are like [[entropic-forces]]: molecules don't know about density across the room, but collectively achieve uniform density. People don't need to be utility-maximizing for supply and demand to work — there are simply more possible states with higher demand at lower prices.

This solves a tough problem: if humans have free will and aren't perfectly rational, why does economics math work so well when markets function? Answer: same reason thermodynamics works — maximum entropy, not maximum utility.

"To turn Tolstoy upside down: *all functioning markets are alike; each market failure fails in its own way.*"

## EMH reinterpretation

Prices are "maximally uninformative" about the microstate — they contain the least information about underlying changes as possible. Not *completely* uninformative (Shiller showed that), but in general, maximum entropy = efficient markets.
