# Keynesian economics in three graphs (2015-01-24)

[Original post](../../raw/2015/01/keynesian-economics-in-three-graphs.md)

A beautifully clear explanation of why Keynesian fiscal policy works in the ITM framework, using statistical mechanics.

## The picture

The economy is made up of many random markets, each in a "growth state." The distribution is centered around the average growth rate, maintained by [[entropic-forces]].

### Distribution of market growth states

![Economic growth state distribution](<../media/2015-01/ite great stagnation 1.png>)

Each square = a firm or market. Distribution centered on average macro growth rate.

### Government (G) as a large block at the average

![Government at average growth](<../media/2015-01/keynesian economcis 1.png>)

Government is ~10–30% of the economy and normally grows at the average rate.

### Government fiscal expansion — moving G rightward

![Fiscal expansion moves G right](<../media/2015-01/keynesian economics 2a.png>)

Government can move to higher growth by political fiat because it's a single entity, not subject to the [[entropic-forces]] that maintain the distribution for small boxes.

## Why crowding out doesn't work

**Crowding out** requires the other boxes to move left (lower growth) to compensate. But that's a **coordination problem** — the remaining smaller boxes can't organize themselves to over-represent low growth rates. That would require a spontaneous decrease in entropy, which is thermodynamically absurd.

**Ricardian equivalence** in this framework is "the rather silly argument that the other molecules decide to get colder on seeing the water molecules heat up (out of spite, I guess)."

## The microwave analogy

Keynesian fiscal policy is like **heating leftovers in the microwave**:
- Food comes out of the fridge at 55°F, molecules distributed by Maxwell-Boltzmann
- Microwave excites rotational modes of water molecules — picking them out for extra energy (like raising G)
- The other molecules eventually reach thermal equilibrium with the new higher average temperature
- The Keynesian multiplier = other markets re-equilibrating to the new distribution

## Significance

This post gives the clearest statistical-mechanical foundation for why fiscal policy works and crowding out doesn't: entropy prevents the rest of the economy from spontaneously coordinating to offset government spending. This is the same [[entropic-forces]] mechanism that explains [[sticky-prices]] and recessions.
