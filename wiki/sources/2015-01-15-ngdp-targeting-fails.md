# Targeting NGDP is awesome (if only it worked) (2015-01-15)

[Part 1](../../raw/2015/01/targeting-ngdp-is-awesome.md) | [Part 2](../../raw/2015/01/targeting-ngdp-is-awesome-if-only-it.md)

A two-part post. Part 1 agrees with Nick Rowe that NGDP level targeting is superior to inflation targeting — it's an obvious choice between targeting *no recessions* and targeting *no deflation during a recession*. But Part 2 shows the ITM is an existence proof that **both eventually fail**.

## The core argument

The price level follows $\log P \sim k \log M0$, so inflation is proportional to money growth. But $k$ changes over time — it **decreases** — so hitting the same inflation target requires printing exponentially more money. The relationship between money growth and inflation/NGDP/RGDP shifts over decades:

### Inflation rate vs. M0 growth rate (1965–2015)

![Inflation vs money growth, shifting over time](<../media/2015-01/ite us price level and ngdp-m0 path.png>)

Purple = 1965, red = 2015. The slope decreases over time. Yellow shows the historical distribution of M0 growth rates — there's a sharp cutoff at ~10% (Treasury almost never prints more than 10% in a year). By 2015, hitting 2% inflation requires >15% M0 growth — unprecedented outside WWII.

### NGDP growth vs. M0 growth rate

![NGDP vs money growth, shifting over time](<../media/2015-01/ite us price level and ngdp-m0 path 2.png>)

Same declining slopes. A 5% NGDP target is achievable in 2015 but would become harder and harder without printing more and more money.

### RGDP growth falls over time

![RGDP growth declining](<../media/2015-01/ite us price level and ngdp-m0 path 3.png>)

RGDP growth trend declines within "normal" money growth ranges. Subtracting this trend reveals RGDP growth is a stationary random process (no unit root) — see [[plucking-model]].

### Oscillations in M0 growth — evidence the Fed is struggling

![M0 growth oscillations increasing](<../media/2015-01/ite us price level and ngdp-m0 path oscillation.png>)

If it's getting harder to steer the economy, you'd expect larger and larger corrections — like a driver over-correcting on an increasingly slippery road. The M0 growth data shows exactly this: widening oscillations, resembling a bifurcation fractal. The good news: these wild fluctuations have a decreasing effect as the economy enters [[information-trap]] conditions.

## Why NGDP targeting is just a "no recession target"

Part 1 shows Nick Rowe's model for NGDP targeting superiority reduces to: "the central bank does whatever it needs to do to hit the target." NGDP level targeting eliminates AD falls — but a recession *is* a fall in AD. So the target is really just "no recessions, please."

## Significance

This is a concrete demonstration of the [[information-trap]] theorem's implications: any market-based monetary policy targeting eventually fails because κ increases over time, requiring exponentially more monetary expansion to achieve the same results. The ITM predicts both inflation targeting and NGDP targeting will eventually become impossible without unprecedented money printing.

Smith draws an analogy to physics: the ITM follows from a fundamental argument ([[information-equilibrium]]), derives from a fundamental symmetry (long-run neutrality of money), and is empirically accurate. These are the same earmarks as successful theoretical predictions in physics (positron, black holes).
