# Currency vs. Reserves: M0 and MB

A critical distinction in the [[information-transfer-model]]: the monetary base (MB) comprises two components with different roles in information transfer.

## The distinction

| | M0 (Currency) | MB (Monetary Base) |
|---|---|---|
| Composition | Currency in circulation | Currency + bank reserves |
| ITM role | Price level detector | Interest rate detector |
| Model | P:NGDP→M0 | r:NGDP→MB |
| Controls | Inflation, long-term rates (10yr) | Short-term rates (3mo) |

## Why it matters

This explains the central puzzle of post-2008 monetary policy: **QE's massive reserve injections had minimal inflation impact**. QE expanded MB enormously (via reserves) but barely affected M0. Since only M0 enters the price level equation, inflation didn't respond.

### The cleanest macro experiment

Post-2008 data shows central bank reserves (MB − M0) are **completely uncorrelated** with inflation. Only M0 remains correlated. This is arguably the cleanest natural experiment in macroeconomics.

## Model residuals

| Model | Mean error |
|---|---|
| ITM with M0 | 0.03 |
| ITM with MB | 0.04 |
| Constant κ | 0.10 |
| QTM | 0.37 |

## Implications

- QE affects short-term rates (through MB) but not prices (which need M0)
- [[inflation]] forecasting should use M0, not MB
- The [[information-trap]] is really about M0/NGDP ratio, not MB/NGDP
- Explains why Japan's massive MB expansion produced no inflation — M0 barely moved
