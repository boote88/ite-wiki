# Three Foundational Ideas of Information Transfer Economics

In November 2013, Smith explicitly formulated the ITM as three building blocks of increasing complexity. This is the clearest statement of the complete framework.

## Idea 1: Supply and Demand as Information Transfer

The "first law": a price detects information transfer from demand (source) to supply (destination):

$$P = \frac{1}{\kappa} \frac{D}{S}$$

This is the [[information-transfer-model]] applied to a single market. It derives the standard [[supply-and-demand]] diagram from information theory without microfoundations.

## Idea 2: Multiple Interacting Markets

The same framework applies to multiple markets using the same or linked price signals:

$$P = \frac{1}{\kappa} \frac{dD}{dS}$$

with solutions depending on whether sources/destinations are exogenous (constant) or endogenous (floating):
- $D \sim S^{1/\kappa}$ (both floating)
- $D \sim \exp S$ (one constant)
- $D \sim \log S$ (limiting cases)

This generates IS-LM, [[ad-as-model]], LS-MS (labor-money) models, and derives relationships like Okun's law.

## Idea 3: Unit of Account Effect

The [[information-transfer-index]] κ is not a constant but **endogenous** to the monetary base and NGDP:

$$\kappa(\text{NGDP}, \text{MB}) = \frac{\log(\text{NGDP}/c_0)}{\log(\text{MB}/c_0)}$$

This makes the monetary base simultaneously a medium of exchange and a unit of account. It creates the [[information-trap]] where adding more money reduces information per dollar (diminishing marginal utility of monetary expansion).

## Significance

Ideas 1 and 2 alone give a useful framework for understanding markets. Idea 3 is what makes the framework *predictive* — it explains why the same monetary policy has different effects in different eras and countries, why the [[quantity-theory-of-money]] works sometimes but not others, and why monetary policy eventually becomes ineffective.
