# Supply and Demand from Information Transfer

A central result of the [[information-transfer-model]]: the traditional supply and demand diagram can be *derived* from information theory rather than assumed as a heuristic.

## Derivation

Starting from the ITM with ideal information transfer ($I_{Q^s} = I_{Q^d}$):

**Demand curve** — hold demand constant at $Q_0^d$ (constant information source), integrate:

$$P = \frac{1}{\kappa} \frac{Q_0^d}{Q^s}$$

The demand curve slopes downward, recovering diminishing marginal utility. This comes from supply appearing in the denominator of the price detector.

**Supply curve** — hold supply constant at $Q_0^s$ (constant information destination):

$$P = \frac{1}{\kappa} \frac{Q^d}{Q_0^s}$$

**Families of curves** are generated by varying $Q_0^d$ or $Q_0^s$.

## Linearization

Taylor-expanding around a reference point $Q_{\text{ref}}^x$ recovers the textbook linear model:

$$Q^d = \alpha - \beta P$$
$$Q^s = \gamma + \delta P$$

where the coefficients $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta$ are determined by $\kappa$, $Q_0$, and $Q_{\text{ref}}$.

## Movement of vs. along curves

- **Movement along** a curve: one side is a constant information source/destination (standard partial equilibrium)
- **Movement of** curves: the information source/destination varies (shifts in supply or demand), corresponding to a "floating" information source in [[fielitz-borchardt]]'s language

## Supply and demand as a symmetry principle

At the deepest level, supply and demand is a consequence of **scale invariance** — the same symmetry that generates conservation laws in physics via Noether's theorem. The [[information-equilibrium]] condition $P = (1/\kappa)(D/S)$ has a conformal symmetry: if $D \to \alpha D$ and $S \to \beta S$, the equation is unchanged (up to a constant scale in $P$). This is the economic analog of homogeneity of degree zero — doubling both supply and demand doesn't change the price.

The parallel to physics is precise. Newton's $F = dp/dt$ was a *definition* that organized disparate empirical observations (Kepler, Galileo) into a coherent framework. The ITM's $P = dD/dS$ is the same kind of definition. Both look like statements of fundamental law but are really recognitions of underlying symmetry. Newton's symmetry was Galilean invariance; the ITM's is scale invariance. Newton's laws are consequences of conservation of momentum (Noether's theorem); supply and demand relationships are consequences of the conservation of the "economic charge" implied by homogeneity of degree zero (see [[fiat-money-paradox]] for the Noether connection).

## Scope conditions

Recognizing S&D as a consequence of scale invariance also gives precise **scope conditions** for when the framework applies and when it breaks. The key requirement: supply must be able to change faster than demand (or vice versa) for the partial equilibrium curves to have meaning. When both adjust at the same rate, you're in general equilibrium and the textbook S&D diagram doesn't apply.

This fails in several important real-world cases:
- **[[labor-market|Labor markets]]**: adding workers adds wage earners who add demand, so supply and demand shift together
- **Housing markets**: it's impossible to add housing units fast enough to outrun the demand response (see [[minimum-wage]] for the parallel argument)
- **Any market where supply and demand are coupled** through income or wealth effects

In these cases, the Econ 101 story (raise minimum wage → unemployment, build more housing → lower prices) is wrong because the scope conditions for partial equilibrium don't hold. The ITM provides the general equilibrium solution ($D \sim S^k$) which applies when S&D adjust together. See [[minimum-wage]] for the detailed application.

## Significance

This derivation means supply and demand are not merely a useful heuristic (as Krugman described them) but emerge naturally from information-theoretic principles. The framework requires only:
1. Two process variables (supply and demand)
2. The ability to transfer information between them
3. A detector (price) measuring the transfer
