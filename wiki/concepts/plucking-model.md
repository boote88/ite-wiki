# Plucking Model of Business Cycles

Smith adopts and formalizes Milton Friedman's "plucking model" within the [[information-transfer-model]]: RGDP growth is bounded from above, with recessions as downward "plucks" from this ceiling.

## ITM interpretation

The bound corresponds to **100% information transfer efficiency**: $I_S = I_D$ (all demand information reaches supply). The economy operates at or below this ceiling:

$$I_S \leq I_D$$

- **At the bound** ($I_S = I_D$): ideal information transfer, maximum output for given inputs
- **Below the bound**: information loss, idle resources, recession
- **Great Moderation** (1984–2007): the US economy operated near the efficiency bound
- **Great Recession**: a sharp drop in information transfer efficiency

## Empirical evidence

Removing recessions from the RGDP growth time series yields a near-normal distribution — recessions are asymmetric shocks from a stable ceiling, not symmetric fluctuations around a mean.

## Connection to other ITM concepts

- [[non-ideal-information-transfer]] provides the mechanism for operating below the bound
- [[sticky-prices]] explain why the economy doesn't instantly return to the bound
- The [[information-trap]] represents a regime where the bound itself is difficult to reach via monetary policy
- The plucks themselves are large [[nominal-shocks]] — extracted as departures of NGDP from the M0-predicted path
- See [[recessions]] for the broader treatment of business cycle dynamics, including the avalanche framing and the Great Recession narrative

## Historical regimes

- Pre-1980: US economy at ~50% efficiency → low inflation but below potential
- Post-1980: near-100% efficiency → "Great Moderation"
- Post-2008: sharp efficiency collapse → "Great Recession"

Absolute calibration of efficiency is unknown, but relative changes are observable and meaningful.
