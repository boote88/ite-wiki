# The 1990s Phase Transition

A collection of economic trends that all changed around the 1990s — assembled as a case for the thesis that the completion of the demographic transition (women entering the workforce) triggered a cascade of structural changes in the US economy.

## The thesis

The non-equilibrium process of women entering the workforce peaked in the 1970s and reached its new equilibrium by the early 1990s. This single demographic process, visible as a logistic shock in the [[dynamic-information-equilibrium]] framework, drove or coincided with changes in nearly every major macro time series. The 1990s is when the US economy shifted from one regime to another — not because of any policy change or technological shock, but because a 30-year demographic transition completed.

## What changed

**[[phillips-curve]] faded.** The apparent inflation-unemployment trade-off tracked women's employment-population ratio. Once that ratio stabilized in the 1990s, the Phillips curve slope fell to statistically insignificant levels.

**Recession dynamics shifted.** Before the 1990s, recessions had a "Phillips curve era" character — cyclic fluctuations of inflation and employment. After the 1990s, the "asset bubble era" began: dot-com boom/bust, housing boom/bust. [[recessions]] became governed by asset price dynamics rather than wage-price spirals.

**Long-term unemployment structure changed.** Before the 1990s, the fraction of long-term unemployed (27+ weeks) rose with recessions and fell back during recoveries. After the 1990s, each recession produced a cumulative increase that didn't fully unwind — a ratchet effect.

**[[inequality]] rose, labor share fell, unions declined.** These trends all completed their major transitions by the end of the 1990s, in roughly that causal order: women entering workforce → manufacturing declining → unionization declining → labor share declining → inequality rising.

**Housing price dynamics changed.** Before the 1990s, housing prices followed the business cycle (falling with recessions). After the 1990s, housing prices decoupled from the business cycle — they didn't fall in the 2001 recession, contributing to the housing bubble and today's affordability crisis. The hypothesis: white flight faded and gentrification began — new segregation through housing prices replacing *de jure* segregation.

**Immigration patterns shifted.** Immigration from Mexico was increasing during the 1990s but started declining afterward. The unauthorized immigration wave of the 1990s may have picked up growth where women's participation left off (see [[quantity-theory-of-labor]]).

**Consumption-to-investment dynamics crystallized.** The ratio of consumption to investment follows a clear [[dynamic-information-equilibrium]] pattern starting in the late 1980s/early 1990s; the period before looks like noise.

**Women's manufacturing employment reversed.** The fraction of women in manufacturing — increasing since the 1960s — began declining sharply in the 1990s, returning to 1960s levels by the Great Recession.

## Social and cultural changes

The economic phase transition was accompanied by (and possibly caused) social changes: violent crime peaked and began declining in the early 1990s (possibly lead-crime hypothesis, possibly related to the demographic transition). The share of violent deaths caused by police began rising after the 1990s. The number of people claiming "American" ancestry on the census doubled between 1990 and 2000. These social indicators suggest the 1990s represented a broader societal inflection, not just an economic one.

## Connection to the framework

The 1990s phase transition is the clearest example of the ITM's central claim: macroeconomic dynamics are driven by **demographic and social forces**, not by monetary policy or technology. The Fed, interest rates, money supply, and policy debates of the era were all riding on top of a demographic wave that was completing its course. Once the wave finished, the economy entered a structurally different regime — lower inflation, asset-bubble-driven recessions, rising inequality, housing price decoupling — none of which were caused by any policy choice and none of which can be reversed by monetary policy.

The [[economic-seismogram]] makes this visible: the same logistic shock (women entering workforce) appears across every time series, and its completion in the 1990s marks the transition from one macro regime to another.
